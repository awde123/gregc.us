Alvin Cullum York 1887-1964

Historical Question Background Religion and the Draft

What impact did Alvin C. York have on East Tennessee?

York had a measurable impact on the world through his couragous actions in The Great War- specifically his triumph in an attack on a German Machine Gun nest for which he recieved the Congressional Medal of Honor. Although he recieved national acclaim, to what extent did he change his community? What is his lasting impact on his hometown?

Background

York came from humble beginnings: a two-room cabin near modern-day Pall Mall, Tennessee. As the third of eleven children from the impoverished Mrs. Mary Elizabeth and Mr. William Uriah York, the entire family had to be focused on sustainability, putting education on the back burner (Alvin only attended school for nine months). In 1911, William York passed, leaving the family in the care of the oldest remaining brother, Alvin (Alvin’s remaining brothers had been married and relocated). The stresses of supporting such a large family led to York’s eventual addiction to alcohol and increasing hostility. Thanks to his mother, he was able to pacify his attitude through a force that would play a major role in the rest of his life: Christianity.

Religion and the Draft

York belonged to The Church of Christ in Christian Union, a protestant congregation which placed an emphasis on the condemnation of violence, opposite York’s prior ideology. Regardless, in June 5th, 1917, York was chosen for the draft and his many attempts of nullification via conscientious objection due to religion were denied. The mandate for York to join the army was certainly an extension of a stand taken by the U.S. Government. Deeply disturbed by his present situation, York was able to obtain a 10 day leave to contemplate the morality of the situation. This led to York’s eventual acceptance of his involvement in The Great War. This would lead to the saving of hundreds of lives, despite York’s initial reservations and perfunctory participation. Religion played an major role in the York’s decisions which would directly affect the state of Tennessee, the United States, and the World.
